Objectives

I'm currently seeking a Python engineering role in open source tooling and/or earth science analytics. As an extrovert who also thrives on deep technical immersion, I'm looking to contribute my skills to challenging and impactful engineering problems while also serving in a community outreach and/or teaching capacity.

Workplaces which value professional development and wellbeing are of the greatest interest to me. Lifelong learning is one of my greatest passions. In this phase of my career, I look forward to dramatically expanding my software engineering expertise as well as my knowledge of adjacent disciplies such as applied math.

While the earth sciences have historically been inexorably linked with extractive industry, I seek to devote my work in this arena to scientific applications which facilitate climate change adaptation & mitigation, renewable energy and resource development, and which spark curiosity, enthusiasm, and stewardship of the natural world.

I will update this page as time goes along, and welcome follow-up conversations on these or related subjects.

Commitments

In his 1962 A Letter to My Nephew, James Baldwin writes:

"To act is to be committed and to be committed is to be in danger."

More recently, Merrick Garland spoke of his indebtedness to the United States of America for sheltering his family from anti-Semitic persecution, and his desire work alongside BIPOC communities to realize equal opportunity for safety, justice, and personal advancement.

As a Jewish American whose own ancestors also found safe haven in the US, Garland's sentiments resonate deeply. Though my position may not be as exalted as his, the priviledge and opportunity I am afforded nonetheless carries with it great responsibility. My participation in Unlearning Racism in Geoscience (URGE) is directed towards crafting a set of commitments with which to hold myself accountable to this work.

Taking an international view, I commit to understanding how my work as a technologist can be of greatest benefit to users in the Global South. This includes understanding accessibility issues such as interface and documentation languages, platforms (desktop vs. mobile), and broadband availability.

Of all the dangers suggested by Baldwin's prescient Letter, the dual risks of inaction and performative "clicktivism" are themselves even more dangerous, both morally and practically. I hope that my future colleagues will hold me accountable to the fact that "inclusivity is an action," should I ever fail to do so myself.

My experience of the software engineering and earth science communities has been entirely positive. I have felt welcomed and supported at every turn. Listening to my non-male-gendered, LGBTQ+, and BIPOC peers teaches me that this experience is by no means universal. Quite to the contrary, many are actively harassed and excluded on the basis of their gender, sexuality, race, or other intersectional identity. This discrimination is a highest order concern for me and I commit to sharing a set of actionable D.E.I. commitments at the conclusion of the URGE curriculum this spring.
